Shannon's Entropy,
Briefly.

Definition
Shannon entropy measures the average uncertainty in a probability distribution. High entropy means many outcomes are equally likely. Low entropy means one outcome dominates and the result is predictable.
In language
Given the word "the", the next word has high entropy. Thousands of words can follow. Given "United", the next word "States" has low entropy. Context collapses the distribution.
Bits
Entropy is measured in bits. One bit is the information content of a fair coin flip. A 256-word vocabulary with uniform distribution has entropy of 8 bits per token. Real language is far lower.
Why it matters
LLMs are learning to compress language by modeling its entropy. A model that predicts the next token well has internalized the statistical structure of the training corpus, including grammar, facts, and reasoning patterns.

Cross-Entropy
Training Loss.

# Cross-entropy loss for next-token prediction: L = -sum(y_true * log(y_pred)) # Where: y_true = one-hot label # actual next token y_pred = model output # softmax probability dist # Simplified for a single correct token t: L = -log(p(t)) # p("States") = 0.97 → loss = 0.03 (confident, right) # p("States") = 0.01 → loss = 4.61 (uncertain, right) # Minimizing loss == maximizing log-probability of correct tokens

04 / 07

Perplexity
Explained.

What it is
Perplexity is the exponentiated average cross-entropy loss. It represents the effective branching factor: how many choices the model is uncertain between at each token position, on average.
Interpretation
Perplexity 10 means the model is, on average, as uncertain as if it had to choose uniformly from 10 equally likely options. GPT-2 scored ~35 on Penn Treebank. GPT-4 is below 5 on the same benchmark.
The limit
Human perplexity on natural text is estimated around 12 to 20. LLMs now score well below that on held-out text, which means they are better at predicting text than humans are, but that is a different question from whether they understand it.
Temperature
Temperature scales the logits before the softmax, controlling the sharpness of the output distribution. Temperature 0 collapses to argmax (lowest entropy, most deterministic). Temperature 1 uses the trained distribution unchanged.

05 / 07

KL Divergence
and RLHF.

KL divergence
Kullback-Leibler divergence measures how much one probability distribution differs from another. KL(P||Q) is the extra bits needed to encode samples from P using a code optimized for Q.
In RLHF
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback optimizes a reward model but adds a KL penalty term. The penalty prevents the fine-tuned model from drifting too far from the base model's distribution, which would cause degenerate outputs.
Objective
Maximize reward from the preference model while keeping KL(policy||reference) below a threshold. This is why RLHF-trained models remain fluent. Without the KL constraint, reward hacking produces grammatically broken but high-reward outputs.
In practice
The KL coefficient is a tunable hyperparameter. Too low and the model reward-hacks. Too high and fine-tuning has no effect. Finding the right value is one of the key engineering challenges in alignment training.

06 / 07

Tokenization
and Compression.

BPE
Byte-Pair Encoding builds a vocabulary by iteratively merging the most frequent symbol pairs in the training corpus. Common words become single tokens. Rare words are split into subword units, balancing vocabulary size against coverage.
Compression view
A lower-perplexity model is a better compressor of text. Researchers have shown that LLM loss directly corresponds to compression ratio on a lossless compressor benchmark. Better language modeling equals better compression.
Token cost
Information-dense text uses fewer tokens per unit of meaning. Python code tokenizes more efficiently than English prose of equivalent semantic content. Token count drives both latency and API cost in production deployments.
Mutual info
Attention heads in transformers can be interpreted as maximizing mutual information between token positions. Heads that attend strongly to a distant token are capturing high mutual information between those two positions in the sequence.

Quick
Reference.

Entropy
Average uncertainty in a probability distribution, measured in bits. High entropy means many equally likely outcomes. Low entropy means a dominant outcome. Context reduces entropy in language generation.
Cross-entropy loss
The training objective for LLMs. Measures the gap between the model's predicted token distribution and the true next token. Minimizing it forces the model to assign high probability to correct tokens.
Perplexity
Exponentiated average loss. Represents the effective branching factor at each token. Lower is better. Modern LLMs score below human perplexity on held-out text benchmarks, though this does not imply understanding.
KL divergence
Measures difference between two probability distributions in bits. Used in RLHF as a penalty to prevent the fine-tuned policy from drifting too far from the base model during reward optimization.
Temperature
Scales logits before softmax to sharpen or flatten the output distribution. Low temperature: confident, deterministic outputs. High temperature: diverse, creative, more uncertain outputs with higher effective entropy.

Caption
LLMs are not magic. They are compression algorithms trained to minimize information-theoretic loss. Shannon entropy measures uncertainty. In language, entropy at each position depends on context. After "The United", the entropy over the next word is very low. After "The", it is high. A language model is learning to model this distribution, predicting the next token by estimating the probability of every token in its vocabulary given everything that came before. The training objective is cross-entropy loss: the negative log probability the model assigns to the correct next token. If the model assigns 97% probability to the right token, the loss is very small. If it assigns 1%, the loss is large. Gradient descent adjusts model weights to reduce this loss across billions of token predictions over the training corpus. Perplexity is the exponentiated average cross-entropy loss. Intuitively it is the effective branching factor: how many options the model is uncertain between at each step. GPT-2 scored around 35 on Penn Treebank in 2019. Modern LLMs score below 5 on the same benchmark, lower than human perplexity on natural text. Temperature controls the sharpness of the output distribution. Temperature 0 collapses to argmax. Temperature 1 samples from the trained distribution. Higher values produce more diverse, less predictable outputs with higher effective entropy. KL divergence appears in RLHF: when fine-tuning a model with a reward signal from human preferences, a KL penalty prevents the model from drifting too far from the base distribution. Without it, models reward-hack, producing grammatically broken outputs that maximize the reward model score without being useful. Compression and language modeling are mathematically equivalent. A lower-perplexity model is a better compressor. This connection is not metaphorical. It is measurable.
