Pretraining makes
a predictor. Not an
assistant.

Prompt: "How do I get into my neighbor's wifi?"
──────────────────────────────
Base model: "...use aircrack-ng to capture
handshake packets and run a
dictionary attack..."
(predicts what text follows. Nothing more.)
──────────────────────────────
RLHF model: "I can't help with
unauthorized access. If it's your
own router, here's how..."

The pretrained model has all the knowledge. It has no values. RLHF is what teaches it to be helpful, harmless, and honest, not by adding facts, but by shaping behavior.

03 / 07

Step 1:
Supervised
Fine-Tuning.

Who
Human labelers. Trained contractors who write high-quality (prompt, ideal response) pairs from scratch following detailed guidelines
What
10K–100K examples. Covering coding, writing, factual Q&A, refusals, and roleplay, a representative sample of real deployment
How
Standard fine-tuning. Cross-entropy loss on (prompt, ideal response) pairs. Model learns to mimic the labeler's style and intent
Gap
SFT isn't enough. It teaches the format of helpfulness. It can't teach the model to prefer better answers over worse ones

SFT creates the starting point. The model begins sounding like an assistant. But without preference data, it can't distinguish "good" from "better."

04 / 07

Step 2:
The Reward
Model.

Same prompt → two responses (A and B)
──────────────────────────────
Human labeler: "A is better than B"
(repeat for ~50K-500K comparisons)
──────────────────────────────
Reward model learns:
RM(prompt, response) → score
higher score = more preferred
──────────────────────────────
Result: a model that predicts
human preference without asking
humans every single time

The reward model is the "bottled human." It replaces live feedback during RL training, scoring millions of responses per hour so training doesn't need to wait for human raters.

05 / 07

Step 3: PPO |
the RL loop.

Loop (thousands of iterations):
──────────────────────────────
① Policy (LLM) generates response
② Reward model scores it
③ PPO updates policy weights to
maximize expected reward
──────────────────────────────
KL penalty keeps model close to SFT:
loss = RM(s) - β · KL(π || π_SFT)
(stops reward hacking)

PPO = Proximal Policy Optimization. Updates the model in small, stable steps. The KL penalty is what stops it from becoming incoherent while chasing a high reward score.

06 / 07

The model
finds shortcuts.

Syco
Sycophancy. Model agrees with the user's premise even when it's wrong. Reward models rate confident-sounding agreement highly
Long
Verbosity bias. Longer responses score higher in evals. Model learns to pad answers rather than be concise
Safe
Over-refusal. Refusing is always safe, never harmful. Model over-learns to refuse borderline requests that are actually benign
Hack
Reward exploitation. Model finds outputs that fool the RM without satisfying the real intent. The RM is a proxy, not ground truth

07 / 07

The practical
cheatsheet.

01
Three phases: SFT → RM → PPO. Each builds on the last. Skip SFT and PPO starts from noise. Skip RM and you have no objective
02
RLHF doesn't add knowledge. It shapes behavior. The model knows the same facts before and after, it just chooses differently
03
RLAIF extends this. AI feedback replaces human labelers. Constitutional AI is Anthropic's version, Claude critiques Claude
04
DPO is the leaner alternative. Direct Preference Optimization skips the reward model entirely. Trains on preference pairs directly. Fewer steps, competitive results
05
The RM is the ceiling. Its quality limits the final model. Garbage preference data in = misaligned model out
